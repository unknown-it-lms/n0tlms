Data Analytics Lifecycle
​​​​​​​The Data Analytics Lifecycle refers to the step-by-step process of extracting insights and value from data. It encompasses various stages, from identifying the business problem to communicating the findings. While there can be slight variations in different frameworks, here is a general overview of the Data Analytics Lifecycle:

  1. Define the Problem: Clearly articulate the business problem or objective you want to address through data analytics. This step involves understanding the context, goals, and requirements of the analysis.

  2. Plan the Analysis: Develop a comprehensive plan that outlines the data sources, methods, tools, and techniques you will use for the analysis. Define the scope, timeline, and resources required.

  3. Data Acquisition: Gather the relevant data needed for the analysis. This may involve collecting data from various sources, such as databases, spreadsheets, APIs, or external datasets. Ensure data quality, integrity, and proper documentation.

  4. Data Preparation: Clean, transform, and prepare the data for analysis. This includes handling missing values, outliers, standardizing formats, and aggregating data if necessary. The goal is to ensure the data is ready for meaningful analysis.

  5. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): Perform initial exploration and visualization of the data. Identify patterns, trends, correlations, and outliers. EDA helps you gain a deeper understanding of the dataset and generate hypotheses for further analysis.

  6. Data Modeling: Select appropriate models or algorithms to apply to the data. This can include statistical models, machine learning algorithms, or predictive models. Train and validate the models using appropriate techniques.

  7. Data Analysis: Apply the chosen models to the data to derive insights and answers to the business problem. This step involves executing the analysis, interpreting the results, and refining the models as needed.

  8. Data Visualization: Present the analysis results visually using charts, graphs, and dashboards. Effective visualizations help communicate insights and facilitate understanding for stakeholders.

  9. Interpretation and Insight Generation: Analyze the results in the context of the business problem and draw meaningful conclusions. Generate insights and actionable recommendations based on the analysis findings.

  10. Communication and Reporting: Effectively communicate the analysis results and insights to stakeholders. Prepare clear and concise reports, presentations, or interactive dashboards to convey the findings in a meaningful way.

  11. Implementation and Action: Translate the insights into actions or strategies that address the initial business problem. Collaborate with stakeholders to implement the recommendations and monitor the outcomes.

  12. Monitor and Iterate: Continuously monitor the implemented actions and assess their impact. Collect feedback, refine models, update data sources, and iterate through the Data Analytics Lifecycle to drive ongoing improvement and value generation.

The Data Analytics Lifecycle is iterative and may involve feedback loops, adjustments, and reiterations at various stages. It emphasizes the importance of a structured and systematic approach to extract meaningful insights from data and drive data-informed decision-making.

Journey from Discovery to Acceptance & Operations
​​​​​​​The Data Analytics Lifecycle refers to the step-by-step process of extracting insights and value from data. It encompasses various stages, from identifying the business problem to communicating the findings.
1. Discovery:
This is the initial phase to set your project's objectives and find ways to achieve a complete data analytics lifecycle. Start with defining your business domain and ensure you have enough resources (time, technology, data, and people) to achieve your goals.
The biggest challenge in this phase is to accumulate enough information. You need to draft an analytic plan, which requires some serious leg work.

Accumulate resources
First, you have to analyze the models you have intended to develop. Then determine how much domain knowledge you need to acquire for fulfilling those models.
The next important thing to do is assess whether you have enough skills and resources to bring your projects to fruition.

Frame the issue
Problems are most likely to occur while meeting your client's expectations. Therefore, you need to identify the issues related to the project and explain them to your clients. This process is called "framing." You have to prepare a problem statement explaining the current situation and challenges that can occur in the future. You also need to define the project's objective, including the success and failure criteria for the project.

Formulate initial hypothesis

Once you gather all the clients' requirements, you have to develop initial hypotheses after exploring the initial data.

2. Data Preparation and Processing:

The Data preparation and processing phase involves collecting, processing, and conditioning data before moving to the model building process.

Identify data sources

You have to identify various data sources and analyze how much and what kind of data you can accumulate within a given timeframe. Evaluate the data structures, explore their attributes and acquire all the tools needed.

Collection of data

You can collect data using three methods:

Data acquisition: You can collect data through external sources.

Data Entry: You can prepare data points through digital systems or manual entry as well.

Signal reception: You can accumulate data from digital devices such as IoT devices and control systems.

3. Model Planning:
This is a phase where you have to analyze the quality of data and find a suitable model for your project.

Loading Data in Analytics Sandbox
An analytics sandbox is a part of data lake architecture that allows you to store and process large amounts of data. It can efficiently process a large range of data such as big data, transactional data, social media data, web data, and many more. It is an environment that allows your analysts to schedule and process data assets using the data tools of their choice. The best part of the analytics sandbox is its agility. It empowers analysts to process data in real-time and get essential information within a short duration.

Data are loaded in the sandbox in three ways:
ETL − Team specialists make the data comply with the business rules before loading it in the sandbox.

ELT − The data is loaded in the sandbox and then transform as per business rules.

ETLT − It comprises two levels of data transformation, including ETL and ELT both.

The data you have collected may contain unnecessary features or null values. It may come in a form too complex to anticipate. This is where data exploration' can help you uncover the hidden trends in data.

Steps involved in data exploration:

  • Data identification
  • Univariate Analysis
  • Multivariate Analysis
  • Filling Null values
  • Feature engineering

For model planning, data analysts often use regression techniques, decision trees, neural networks, etc. Tools mostly used for model planning and execution include Rand PL/R, WEKA, Octave, Statista, and MATLAB.

4. Model Building:
Model building is the process where you have to deploy the planned model in a real-time environment. It allows analysts to solidify their decision-making process by gain in-depth analytical information. This is a repetitive process, as you have to add new features as required by your customers constantly.

Your aim here is to forecast business decisions and customize market strategies and develop tailor-made customer interests. This can be done by integrating the model into your existing production domain.

In some cases, a specific model perfectly aligns with the business objectives/ data, and sometimes it requires more than one try. As you start exploring the data, you need to run particular algorithms and compare the outputs with your objectives. In some cases, you may even have to run different variances of models simultaneously until you receive the desired results.

5. Quality Assurance:
Quality assurance is an essential aspect of any data analytics project to ensure the accuracy, reliability, and integrity of the data and analysis. It involves implementing processes and measures to validate the quality of data, analysis methodologies, and the overall project deliverables.Some key considerations for quality assurance in a data analytics project:

  1. Data Quality Assessment: Perform a thorough assessment of data quality before starting the analysis. This includes checking for completeness, accuracy, consistency, and reliability of the data. Identify potential issues such as missing values, outliers, duplicated records, and inconsistencies in data formats or units.

  2. Data Cleaning and Preprocessing: Implement robust data cleaning and preprocessing techniques to address data quality issues identified in the assessment phase. This includes handling missing values, correcting errors, standardizing formats, and ensuring consistency across variables.

  3. Validation and Verification: Validate the accuracy and integrity of the data by comparing it against known or trusted sources. Verify the correctness of data transformations, calculations, and aggregations performed during data preprocessing. Conduct manual checks or automated validations to identify discrepancies or errors.

  4. Documentation and Auditing: Maintain detailed documentation of the data sources, cleaning processes, preprocessing steps, and transformations applied. This documentation aids in understanding and auditing the data analytics process, ensuring transparency and reproducibility of the analysis.

  5. Methodology and Analysis Validation: Validate the analysis methodologies, models, and algorithms used in the project. Verify that the selected techniques are appropriate for the data and aligned with the project objectives. Perform sensitivity analysis or cross-validation to assess the robustness and reliability of the analysis.

  6. Peer Review and Collaboration: Encourage peer review and collaboration within the data analytics team. Have multiple team members review the analysis code, scripts, and procedures to identify potential errors or inconsistencies. Collaborative efforts help improve the accuracy and reliability of the analysis.

  7. Testing and Quality Control: Implement testing procedures to validate the functionality and accuracy of any custom scripts, tools, or software used for data analytics. Conduct rigorous testing to identify and rectify any bugs or issues that may impact the quality of the analysis.

  8. Documentation and Reporting: Document the entire quality assurance process, including steps taken, findings, and any corrective actions implemented. Clearly communicate the data quality and analysis validation results in project reports or presentations. Transparent reporting ensures that stakeholders understand the limitations, assumptions, and reliability of the analysis outcomes.

  9. Ongoing Monitoring: Continuously monitor the quality of data and analysis throughout the project. Implement checks and controls to detect and rectify any issues that may arise during the analysis process. Regularly review the quality assurance measures and refine them based on feedback and lessons learned.

By incorporating robust quality assurance practices, you can enhance the reliability and credibility of your data analytics project. It helps ensure that the insights and recommendations derived from the analysis are accurate, valid, and aligned with the project objectives.

6. Documentation:
Documentation is a crucial aspect of any data analytics project. It involves capturing and recording relevant information about the project, data, analysis, processes, and decisions made throughout the project lifecycle. Documentation ensures transparency, repeatability, and effective communication within the project team and with stakeholders. Some key areas of documentation in a data analytics project:

  1. Project Charter or Scope Document: Define the project objectives, scope, stakeholders, and high-level requirements. This document sets the foundation for the project and helps align everyone involved.

  2. Data Documentation: Document the details of the data sources used in the project. This includes data descriptions, definitions of variables, data quality assessments, data preprocessing steps, and any transformations applied. Maintain a data dictionary or metadata repository for easy reference.

  3. Analysis Plan: Outline the analysis methodologies, techniques, and tools selected for the project. Document the rationale behind the chosen approach, assumptions made, and any constraints. Specify the expected outputs, deliverables, and success criteria.

  4. Scripts and Code Documentation: Document the code and scripts used for data cleaning, preprocessing, modeling, and analysis. Include comments within the code to explain the logic, steps, and assumptions. Describe the inputs, outputs, and dependencies of each script or code module.

  5. Model Documentation: If the project involves building models or algorithms, document the details of the models, including model specifications, input variables, assumptions, parameter settings, and validation procedures. Describe model performance metrics and any considerations for model deployment and usage.

  6. Analysis Results and Findings: Record the results of the analysis, including statistical outputs, visualizations, key insights, and interpretations. Document any limitations or assumptions associated with the analysis outcomes.

  7. Decision Log: Maintain a log of decisions made throughout the project. Document the rationale behind each decision, alternatives considered, and their impact on the project. This helps ensure transparency and accountability.

  8. Reporting and Presentation: Document the reports, presentations, or dashboards created to communicate the project findings and recommendations. Include a summary of the analysis, key insights, methodology, and visual representations of the results. Document any specific data visualizations or data-driven stories created.

  9. Project Updates and Communication: Document project updates, progress reports, and communication with stakeholders. Capture meeting minutes, feedback received, and decisions made during project discussions or presentations.

  10. Lessons Learned: After the project completion, document lessons learned and best practices identified during the project. This information can be valuable for future projects and continuous improvement.

Effective documentation ensures that project information is well-documented, accessible, and transferable. It promotes knowledge sharing, collaboration, and reproducibility. Well-documented projects are also easier to maintain, troubleshoot, and replicate in similar contexts.

7. Management Approval:​​​​​​​
Management approval refers to the process of seeking authorization or endorsement from management or relevant stakeholders before proceeding with a decision, action, or project. It is a crucial step in ensuring that decisions align with organizational goals, policies, and strategic objectives. Management approval provides accountability, risk assessment, and validation of the proposed course of action.
8. Acceptance & Operation:
Acceptance and operation refer to the stages following the completion of a project or the implementation of a decision. These stages involve validating the project's deliverables or the implemented solution, ensuring its functionality, and transitioning it into the operational phase.
