Hypothesis Techniques:

Hypothesis testing involves applying specific techniques to analyze sample data and make inferences about population parameters. The choice of technique depends on factors such as the research question, the nature of the data, and the type of hypotheses being tested. Here are some commonly used hypothesis testing techniques:

  1. Student's t-test: The t-test is used to test hypotheses about the means of two independent samples or the difference between paired samples. It is commonly employed when comparing means of two groups, such as a treatment group and a control group.

  2. Analysis of Variance (ANOVA): ANOVA is used to test hypotheses about the means of three or more independent groups. It determines whether there are significant differences among the means of multiple groups and helps identify which specific groups differ from each other.

  3. Chi-square test: The chi-square test is used to test hypotheses about the association or independence of categorical variables. It assesses whether there is a significant difference between observed and expected frequencies in contingency tables.

  4. Regression analysis: Regression analysis is used to test hypotheses about the relationships between variables. It helps determine if there is a significant linear relationship between an independent variable(s) and a dependent variable.

  5. Mann-Whitney U test: The Mann-Whitney U test is a non-parametric test used to compare two independent samples when the data do not meet the assumptions of normality or homogeneity of variance. It is an alternative to the t-test.

  6. Wilcoxon signed-rank test: The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is a non-parametric test used to compare paired samples when the data do not meet the assumptions of normality. It assesses whether there is a significant difference between the paired observations.

  7. Kruskal-Wallis test: The Kruskal-Wallis test is a non-parametric alternative to ANOVA. It is used to test hypotheses about the medians of three or more independent groups when the data violate the assumptions of normality or homogeneity of variance.

  8. Spearman's rank correlation: Spearman's rank correlation is a non-parametric measure of the strength and direction of the monotonic relationship between two variables. It is used when the relationship between variables is not necessarily linear or when the data violate assumptions of normality.

Statistical Inference Terminology:
​​​​​​​Statistical inference involves drawing conclusions or making predictions about a population based on sample data. It utilizes various statistical techniques and measures to analyze the sample and make inferences about the larger population. Following are some key statistical inference terminologies:

  1. Population: In statistics, a population refers to the entire group or set of individuals, objects, or observations of interest. It represents the larger target group about which inferences are to be made.

  2. Sample: A sample is a subset of the population that is selected and studied to gather information. The goal of sampling is to obtain representative and unbiased information about the population.

  3. Parameter: A parameter is a numerical characteristic of a population. It represents a summary measure or feature of the entire population, such as the mean, standard deviation, proportion, correlation coefficient, or regression coefficient.

  4. Statistic: A statistic is a numerical measure or summary calculated from sample data. It provides an estimate or approximation of the corresponding population parameter. Examples of statistics include the sample mean, sample standard deviation, sample proportion, and sample correlation coefficient.

  5. Estimation: Estimation involves using sample data to estimate or approximate unknown population parameters. Point estimation involves calculating a single value that is considered the best guess for the parameter. Interval estimation provides a range of values within which the parameter is believed to lie, along with a level of confidence.

  6. Confidence Interval: A confidence interval is a range of values constructed around a point estimate of a parameter. It provides an interval estimate along with a level of confidence that the true parameter value falls within the interval. For example, a 95% confidence interval implies that if the sampling process were repeated multiple times, 95% of the intervals constructed would contain the true parameter value.

  7. Hypothesis Testing: Hypothesis testing is a procedure to assess the evidence in the sample data and make inferences about the population. It involves formulating a null hypothesis (H0) and an alternative hypothesis (Ha) and using statistical tests to determine if there is sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis in favor of the alternative hypothesis.

  8. Type I and Type II Errors: In hypothesis testing, a Type I error occurs when the null hypothesis is wrongly rejected, suggesting an effect or difference when none exists in the population. A Type II error occurs when the null hypothesis is incorrectly retained, failing to detect a true effect or difference in the population.

  9. p-value: The p-value is the probability of obtaining a test statistic as extreme as, or more extreme than, the observed value, assuming the null hypothesis is true. It provides a measure of the strength of evidence against the null hypothesis. A smaller p-value indicates stronger evidence against the null hypothesis.

  10. Significance Level (α): The significance level, often denoted by α, is the predetermined threshold used to make decisions in hypothesis testing. It represents the maximum acceptable probability of making a Type I error. Common significance levels include 0.05 (5%) and 0.01 (1%).

Hypothesis Testing:
​​​​​​​Hypothesis testing is a statistical procedure used to make inferences about a population based on sample data. It involves formulating two competing hypotheses, the null hypothesis (H0) and the alternative hypothesis (Ha), and assessing the evidence in the sample to determine which hypothesis is more plausible.

The null hypothesis (H0) represents a statement of no effect, no difference, or no relationship in the population. It is often the hypothesis that researchers seek to challenge or reject. The alternative hypothesis (Ha) represents the opposite of the null hypothesis and suggests that there is an effect, difference, or relationship in the population.

The hypothesis testing procedure involves the following steps:

  1. Formulating the hypotheses:

    • H0 (null hypothesis): States the absence of an effect, difference, or relationship.
    • Ha (alternative hypothesis): States the presence of an effect, difference, or relationship.

  2. Selecting a significance level (α):

    • The significance level, often denoted by α, determines the threshold for deciding whether to reject the null hypothesis. It represents the probability of observing a result as extreme as, or more extreme than, the one obtained from the sample, assuming that the null hypothesis is true.

  3. Collecting and analyzing the data:

    • Data is collected from a sample or samples, and statistical analysis is performed to estimate population parameters, calculate test statistics, and evaluate the evidence against the null hypothesis.

  4. Choosing an appropriate test statistic:

    • The choice of the test statistic depends on the nature of the data and the research question. Common test statistics include t-tests, chi-square tests, ANOVA, correlation coefficients, and regression coefficients.

  5. Calculating the p-value:

    • The p-value is the probability of obtaining a test statistic as extreme as, or more extreme than, the observed value, assuming the null hypothesis is true. It provides a measure of the strength of evidence against the null hypothesis. A smaller p-value suggests stronger evidence against the null hypothesis.

  6. Making a decision:

    • Compare the p-value to the chosen significance level (α). If the p-value is less than α, the evidence is considered statistically significant, and the null hypothesis is rejected in favor of the alternative hypothesis. If the p-value is greater than or equal to α, there is not enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis.

  7. Drawing conclusions:

    • Based on the decision, conclusions are drawn regarding the presence or absence of an effect, difference, or relationship in the population. It is important to interpret the results in the context of the research question and the limitations of the study.
Parametric Tests:
​​​​​​​Parametric tests are statistical tests that make assumptions about the underlying distribution of the data being analyzed. These tests are commonly used when certain conditions are met, such as data being normally distributed or having a known distribution. Parametric tests provide more precise estimates and often have higher statistical power compared to non-parametric tests.
1. t-test:
The t-test is a parametric statistical test used to compare the means of two independent groups or to test the difference between the mean of a single group and a known value. It is commonly used when working with continuous numerical data and assumes that the data are normally distributed.

There are different types of t-tests depending on the specific research question and study design. Here, we'll cover two common scenarios: the independent samples t-test and the paired samples t-test.

i) Independent Samples T-Test:
The independent samples t-test compares the means of two independent groups to determine if they are significantly different. The null hypothesis (H0) assumes that there is no difference between the group means, while the alternative hypothesis (Ha) suggests that there is a significant difference.

Example of performing an independent samples t-test in Python using the SciPy library:
# Import Required Libraries
import scipy.stats as stats

# Sample data for two groups
group1 = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]
group2 = [6, 7, 8, 9, 10]

# Perform independent samples t-test
t_statistic, p_value = stats.ttest_ind(group1, group2)

# Print the results
print("T-Statistic:", t_statistic)
print("P-Value:", p_value)

In this example, we have two groups, group1 and group2, with their respective data. The ttest_ind function from SciPy's stats module is used to perform the independent samples t-test. The function returns the t-statistic and the p-value.
ii) Paired Samples T-Test:
The paired samples t-test is used when working with paired or related observations. It compares the means of two variables measured on the same sample to determine if there is a significant difference between them. The null hypothesis (H0) assumes that the mean difference is zero, while the alternative hypothesis (Ha) suggests that there is a significant difference.
Example of performing a paired samples t-test in Python using the SciPy library:

# Import Required Libraries
import scipy.stats as stats

# Sample data for two paired variables
before = [10, 15, 12, 18, 20]
after = [8, 14, 11, 16, 19]

# Perform paired samples t-test
t_statistic, p_value = stats.ttest_rel(before, after)

# Print the results
print("T-Statistic:", t_statistic)
print("P-Value:", p_value)

In this example, we have two paired variables, before and after, representing measurements before and after a treatment or intervention. The ttest_rel function from SciPy's stats module is used to perform the paired samples t-test. The function returns the t-statistic and the p-value.

In both examples, the t-statistic measures the size and direction of the difference between the group means or paired observations. The p-value represents the probability of observing such a large or larger difference by chance, assuming the null hypothesis is true. A smaller p-value suggests stronger evidence against the null hypothesis.
2. ANOVA Test:
ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) is a parametric statistical test used to compare the means of three or more independent groups. It assesses whether there are significant differences among the group means and helps identify which specific groups differ from each other. ANOVA is commonly used when working with continuous numerical data and assumes that the data are normally distributed.

Example of performing an ANOVA test in Python using the SciPy library:

# Import Required Libraries
import scipy.stats as stats

# Sample data for three groups
group1 = [10, 15, 12, 18, 20]
group2 = [8, 14, 11, 16, 19]
group3 = [5, 9, 7, 13, 17]

# Perform one-way ANOVA
f_statistic, p_value = stats.f_oneway(group1, group2, group3)

# Print the results
print("F-Statistic:", f_statistic)
print("P-Value:", p_value)

In this example, we have three independent groups, group1, group2, and group3, with their respective data. The f_oneway function from SciPy's stats module is used to perform the one-way ANOVA test. The function returns the F-statistic and the p-value.

​​​​​​​The F-statistic measures the ratio of the between-group variability to the within-group variability. It indicates the extent of differences among the group means. The p-value represents the probability of observing such a large or larger F-statistic by chance, assuming the null hypothesis is true. A smaller p-value suggests stronger evidence against the null hypothesis.
Non-parametric Tests:
​​​​​​​Non-parametric tests, also known as distribution-free tests, are statistical tests that do not make any assumptions about the underlying distribution of the data being analyzed. These tests are useful when the data does not meet the assumptions required for parametric tests, such as normality or equal variances.
1. Chi-square Test:
The chi-square test is a statistical test used to determine if there is a significant association between two categorical variables. It is based on the chi-square statistic, which measures the discrepancy between the observed frequencies and the expected frequencies under the assumption of independence.

Step-by-step guide on how to perform a chi-square test:

  1. Set up hypotheses:

    • Null hypothesis (H0): There is no association between the two categorical variables.
    • Alternative hypothesis (Ha): There is an association between the two categorical variables.

  2. Collect and organize data:

    • Tabulate the data into a contingency table, also known as a cross-tabulation or a contingency matrix. The table shows the frequencies or counts of observations in each combination of categories for the two variables.

  3. Calculate expected frequencies:

    • Under the assumption of independence, calculate the expected frequencies for each cell in the contingency table. The expected frequency for a cell is calculated as (row total * column total) / grand total.

  4. Calculate the chi-square test statistic:

    • Compute the chi-square test statistic by summing the squared differences between the observed and expected frequencies for each cell, divided by the expected frequency.
    • The formula for the chi-square statistic is: chi-square = Σ((O - E)^2 / E), where O is the observed frequency and E is the expected frequency for each cell.

  5. Determine the degrees of freedom (df):

    • The degrees of freedom in a chi-square test are calculated as (number of rows - 1) * (number of columns - 1).

  6. Determine the critical value or p-value:

    • Consult a chi-square distribution table or use statistical software to find the critical value corresponding to the desired significance level and degrees of freedom.
    • Alternatively, you can calculate the p-value associated with the chi-square statistic. The p-value represents the probability of observing a test statistic as extreme as the one computed, assuming the null hypothesis is true.

  7. Make a decision:

    • Compare the computed chi-square test statistic to the critical value or compare the p-value to the chosen significance level (e.g., 0.05).
    • If the computed statistic is greater than the critical value or the p-value is less than the significance level, reject the null hypothesis. It suggests evidence of an association between the variables.
    • If the computed statistic is smaller than the critical value or the p-value is greater than the significance level, fail to reject the null hypothesis. It suggests no evidence of an association between the variables.

The chi-square test is commonly used in various fields, such as social sciences, market research, and biology, to analyze categorical data and examine relationships between variables.

An example of performing a chi-square test in Python using the SciPy library:

# Import Required Libraries
import scipy.stats as stats
import numpy as np

# Observed frequencies in a contingency table
observed = np.array([[30, 20, 10],
                     [15, 25, 35]])

# Perform chi-square test
chi2_statistic, p_value, dof, expected = stats.chi2_contingency(observed)

# Print the results
print("Chi-square Statistic:", chi2_statistic)
print("P-Value:", p_value)
print("Degrees of Freedom:", dof)
print("Expected Frequencies:\n", expected)

In this example, we have a contingency table represented by the observed array. Each row corresponds to a category of one variable, and each column corresponds to a category of the other variable. The chi2_contingency function from SciPy's stats module is used to perform the chi-square test. The function returns the chi-square statistic, the p-value, the degrees of freedom, and the expected frequencies.
2. U-test:
The Mann-Whitney U test is a non-parametric test used to compare the distributions of two independent samples. It assesses whether the two samples come from populations with the same distribution or if one sample tends to have higher values than the other.

An example of performing the Mann-Whitney U test in Python using the SciPy library:

# Import Required Libraries
​​​​​​​import scipy.stats as stats

# Sample data for two independent groups
group1 = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]
group2 = [6, 7, 8, 9, 10]

# Perform Mann-Whitney U test
u_statistic, p_value = stats.mannwhitneyu(group1, group2)

# Print the results
print("U-Statistic:", u_statistic)
print("P-Value:", p_value)

In this example, we have two independent groups, group1 and group2, with their respective data. The mannwhitneyu function from SciPy's stats module is used to perform the Mann-Whitney U test. The function returns the U-statistic and the p-value.

The U-statistic represents the sum of ranks assigned to one of the groups. It measures the likelihood that a randomly chosen observation from one group will have a higher rank than a randomly chosen observation from the other group. The p-value represents the probability of observing such a large or larger U-statistic by chance, assuming that the two groups are from populations with the same distribution. A smaller p-value suggests evidence that the two groups have different distributions.​​​​​​​
