Sampling Techniques:
​​​​​​​Sampling techniques are methods used to select a subset of individuals or data points from a larger population or dataset. These techniques are widely employed in various fields, including statistics, market research, surveys, and data analysis. Some common sampling techniques: 

  1. Simple Random Sampling: In this technique, each member of the population has an equal chance of being selected. A random number generator or a table of random numbers is often used to ensure randomness.

  2. Stratified Sampling: This method involves dividing the population into homogeneous subgroups called strata and then randomly selecting samples from each stratum in proportion to their representation in the population. This helps ensure that the sample reflects the diversity within the population.

  3. Cluster Sampling: Cluster sampling involves dividing the population into groups or clusters, and then randomly selecting entire clusters to be included in the sample. This method is useful when it is difficult or impractical to create a complete list of individuals in the population.

  4. Systematic Sampling: In systematic sampling, the researcher selects every nth individual from the population after randomly selecting a starting point. The value of "n" is determined by dividing the population size by the desired sample size.

  5. Convenience Sampling: Convenience sampling involves selecting individuals who are readily available or easily accessible. This method is quick and convenient but may introduce bias, as it may not represent the entire population accurately.

  6. Purposive Sampling: Purposive sampling, also known as judgmental or selective sampling, involves handpicking specific individuals or data points based on their unique characteristics or relevance to the research objective. This technique is subjective and relies on the researcher's judgment.

  7. Snowball Sampling: Snowball sampling is used when it is challenging to identify or reach individuals in the population. The researcher begins by selecting a few individuals who meet the criteria, and then asks them to refer others who fit the criteria. This process continues, and the sample size grows like a snowball.

  8. Quota Sampling: Quota sampling involves setting specific quotas based on predetermined characteristics, such as age, gender, or occupation. Researchers then select individuals who meet these quotas until they are filled. This method is similar to stratified sampling but does not involve random selection.

It's important to note that the choice of sampling technique depends on various factors, including the research objective, available resources, population size, and desired level of accuracy. Researchers must carefully select the appropriate sampling technique to obtain reliable and representative results.

Population & Sample:​​​​​​​
  • In statistics, a population refers to the entire group or collection of individuals, items, or events that researchers are interested in studying. It is the complete set of elements that share certain characteristics or meet specific criteria. The population can be finite, such as the number of students in a school, or infinite, such as the possible outcomes of a dice roll.
  • On the other hand, a sample is a subset or smaller representative selection taken from the population. It is used to gather data and make inferences about the population. The sample should ideally be chosen in such a way that it accurately represents the characteristics of the population.
Population:
  • Population refers to the entire group or collection of individuals, items, or events that researchers are interested in studying. It is the complete set of elements that share certain characteristics or meet specific criteria. The population can be finite or infinite, depending on the context.
  • For example, if researchers are studying the average height of all adults in a country, the population would be the entire adult population of that country. In other cases, the population may be more specific, such as all students in a particular school or all employees in a company.
  • It is important to note that the population is the target of interest for researchers. However, in many practical situations, it is not possible to study the entire population directly. Therefore, researchers select and study samples to gain insights and draw conclusions about the population.
  • Careful consideration is given to sampling methods, sample sizes, and the generalizability of the findings to ensure that valid inferences can be made about the population based on the sample data.

​
Sample:
  • In statistics, a sample refers to a subset of individuals, items, or events selected from a larger population.
  • It is a smaller, manageable group that is studied or analyzed to draw conclusions about the population of interest.
  • Samples are used when studying the entire population is impractical or not feasible due to factors such as time, cost, or logistical constraints.
  • Samples play a crucial role in statistical analysis, as they provide a manageable subset of data that can be used to draw conclusions about larger populations.
  • The quality and validity of the inferences depend on the representativeness of the sample, the appropriate sampling methods used, and the proper application of statistical techniques.

Example of Population and Sample:​​​​​
Let's say you are interested in studying the voting preferences of registered voters in a specific country. The population in this case would be all the registered voters in that country, which could be millions of individuals. This population consists of every person who is eligible to vote and is registered to do so.

To gather data for your study, you could conduct surveys or interviews with a sample of registered voters. However, it's important to note that the sample you select should be representative of the entire population of registered voters. This means that the sample should include individuals from different age groups, genders, regions, and socio-economic backgrounds, among other factors.

By studying a representative sample and analyzing the data collected, you can draw conclusions about the voting preferences of the entire population of registered voters in that country. The sample acts as a smaller, manageable group that represents the larger population, allowing you to make inferences and generalize your findings to the entire population of registered voters.​​​​​

Sampling and Estimation:
Sampling and estimation are fundamental concepts in statistics that are closely related to each other.
Sampling:
  • Sampling refers to the process of selecting a subset of individuals or objects from a larger population for study.
  • The purpose of sampling is to gather data from a more manageable and feasible group, rather than studying the entire population.
  • By selecting a representative sample, one can draw conclusions and make inferences about the larger population.
  • There are different sampling techniques, such as simple random sampling, stratified sampling, cluster sampling, and systematic sampling. Each technique has its own advantages and is chosen based on the specific research objectives, available resources, and constraints.
​
1. Simple Random Sampling:

Simple random sampling is a basic and widely used sampling technique in statistics. It is a method of selecting a sample from a population where each individual or object in the population has an equal chance of being included in the sample.

This is how simple random sampling works:

  1. Define the Population: Clearly define the population of interest, which is the entire group of individuals or objects you want to study.

  2. Define the Sampling Frame: Create a sampling frame, which is a list or representation of all the individuals or objects in the population. The sampling frame should include every member of the population and ensure that each member has a unique identifier.

  3. Determine the Sample Size: Determine the desired sample size, which represents the number of individuals or objects you want to include in your sample. The sample size should be based on statistical considerations, such as desired level of precision and confidence.

  4. Assign Random Numbers: Assign random numbers to each member of the sampling frame. Random numbers can be generated using random number tables, computer software, or other randomization techniques. Each member should have a unique random number.

  5. Select the Sample: Use the assigned random numbers to select the sample. For example, if your desired sample size is 100, you would randomly select 100 individuals or objects from the sampling frame based on their random numbers.

  6. Collect Data: Once the sample is selected, collect the necessary data or measurements from the chosen individuals or objects. This data will be used for analysis and inference about the population.

Simple random sampling ensures that each member of the population has an equal probability of being selected, which helps in obtaining a representative sample. It reduces bias and allows for generalization of the findings from the sample to the larger population.

It's important to note that simple random sampling assumes that the sampling frame is complete and accurate, and that there are no hidden patterns or clusters in the population. Additionally, it can be time-consuming and costly to implement when dealing with large populations.

2. Stratified Sampling:

Stratified sampling is a sampling technique that involves dividing the population into distinct subgroups or strata based on certain characteristics, and then selecting a sample from each stratum. This method ensures that the sample represents the diversity within the population, allowing for more accurate and precise estimates for specific subgroups of interest.

This is how stratified sampling works:

  1. Define the Population: Clearly define the population of interest.

  2. Identify Stratification Variables: Determine the characteristics or variables that are important to the research objectives. These variables should divide the population into mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive strata. For example, if studying a population of students, the stratification variables could be grade level, gender, or socioeconomic status.

  3. Divide the Population into Strata: Categorize the population into different strata based on the identified stratification variables. Each individual in the population should belong to only one stratum, and each stratum should be internally homogeneous while being different from other strata. For example, if stratifying by grade level, the population would be divided into separate strata for each grade.

  4. Determine Sample Sizes: Decide on the sample size for each stratum. The sample size can be determined proportionally based on the size of each stratum relative to the population or can be allocated in a way that ensures a sufficient representation of smaller strata.

  5. Select the Sample from Each Stratum: Randomly select the desired number of individuals or objects from each stratum independently. This can be done using simple random sampling within each stratum or other appropriate sampling techniques.

  6. Combine the Samples: Combine the samples from each stratum to form the overall sample for analysis. The combined sample will represent the diversity of the population and provide more accurate estimates for each stratum.

Stratified sampling has several advantages. It allows for separate analysis and estimation within each stratum, which can provide more detailed insights into specific subgroups of interest. It also ensures that rare or underrepresented groups are adequately represented in the sample, reducing the risk of bias.

However, stratified sampling requires prior knowledge of the population characteristics and stratification variables. Additionally, it may be more complex and time-consuming than simple random sampling, especially when dealing with large populations and numerous strata.

3. Systematic Sampling:

Systematic sampling is a sampling technique that involves selecting every nth individual or object from a population after a random starting point is determined. It is a simple and efficient method of sampling that provides a representative sample when the population has some inherent order or structure.

This is how systematic sampling works:

  1. Define the Population: Clearly define the population of interest.

  2. Determine the Sampling Interval: Determine the sampling interval by dividing the population size by the desired sample size. For example, if the population size is 1,000 and the desired sample size is 100, the sampling interval would be 10 (1,000/100 = 10).

  3. Randomly Select a Starting Point: Randomly select a number between 1 and the sampling interval as the starting point. This random starting point ensures that the sample is not biased towards any specific portion of the population.

  4. Select the Sample: Begin at the randomly selected starting point and select every nth individual or object from the population, where n is the sampling interval. For example, if the starting point is 7 and the sampling interval is 10, you would select individuals with serial numbers 7, 17, 27, and so on.

  5. Continue Selection: Continue selecting every nth individual or object until the desired sample size is reached. If the end of the population is reached before the sample size is achieved, loop back to the beginning of the population and continue the selection.

Systematic sampling is advantageous because it is relatively easy to implement and is less time-consuming than simple random sampling when dealing with large populations. It also provides a representative sample when there is a pattern or structure in the population.

However, systematic sampling has limitations. If there is a cyclical or repeating pattern in the population, the sample may not be fully representative. Additionally, if the order in which individuals or objects appear in the population is related to the variable being studied, it can introduce bias into the sample.

4. Cluster Sampling:

Cluster sampling is a sampling technique that involves dividing a population into groups or clusters and selecting a subset of clusters for inclusion in the sample. It is commonly used when it is impractical or too costly to sample individuals directly from the population.

This is how cluster sampling works:

  1. Define the Population: Clearly define the population of interest.

  2. Create Clusters: Divide the population into non-overlapping clusters or groups based on a specific criterion, such as geographical location, organizational units, or other natural groupings. The clusters should be internally heterogeneous but externally similar.

  3. Determine the Cluster Size: Determine the number of clusters to include in the sample. This can be based on practical considerations such as cost, time, and available resources.

  4. Select Clusters: Randomly select a subset of clusters from the population. This can be done using simple random sampling or other appropriate randomization techniques. It is important to ensure that each cluster has an equal chance of being selected.

  5. Include All Members of Selected Clusters: Once the clusters are selected, include all members or objects within the selected clusters in the sample. This is different from other sampling techniques where only a subset of individuals within the selected clusters is included.

  6. Collect Data: Collect data or measurements from all individuals or objects within the selected clusters. This data will be used for analysis and inference about the population.

Cluster sampling is useful when the population is large, widely dispersed, or difficult to access. It reduces the costs and time required for data collection by sampling groups of individuals instead of each individual separately. It is also helpful when studying clusters as units of analysis, such as schools, hospitals, or communities.

However, cluster sampling introduces a certain level of variability within clusters and may lead to an increased sampling error compared to other sampling techniques. It is important to account for the cluster design during the data analysis phase by using appropriate statistical methods that take the clustering structure into consideration.

5. Uni-variate Sampling:
Univariate sampling refers to a sampling technique where data is collected from a single variable or attribute of interest. In univariate sampling, the focus is on gathering information about a specific characteristic or measurement from a population.

This is how univariate sampling works:

  1. Define the Population: Clearly define the population from which you want to collect data.

  2. Determine the Sample Size: Decide on the desired sample size, which represents the number of observations you want to include in your sample. The sample size should be determined based on statistical considerations, such as desired level of precision and confidence.

  3. Select the Sampling Method: Choose an appropriate sampling method based on the population and the available resources. Common sampling methods for univariate sampling include simple random sampling, stratified sampling, systematic sampling, or cluster sampling.

  4. Collect Data: Once the sampling method is determined, collect data from the chosen individuals or objects in the population. This could involve conducting surveys, interviews, or collecting data from existing sources.

  5. Analyze the Data: Analyze the collected data using appropriate statistical techniques. This may involve calculating summary statistics, such as mean, median, mode, variance, or conducting hypothesis tests or other statistical analyses specific to the variable of interest.

Univariate sampling allows researchers to study and analyze a single variable in isolation, providing insights into its distribution, central tendency, variability, or other characteristics. It is commonly used in various fields, including market research, social sciences, healthcare, and quality control, where the focus is often on understanding and analyzing individual variables.

6. Bi-variate Sampling:

Bivariate sampling refers to a sampling technique where data is collected from two variables or attributes simultaneously. The goal of bivariate sampling is to examine the relationship or association between the two variables of interest.

This is how bivariate sampling works:

  1. Define the Population: Clearly define the population from which you want to collect data.

  2. Identify the Variables: Determine the two variables or attributes of interest that you want to study and collect data on. These variables should have a plausible relationship or potential association.

  3. Determine the Sample Size: Decide on the desired sample size, which represents the number of observations you want to include in your sample. The sample size should be determined based on statistical considerations, such as desired level of precision and confidence, and taking into account the potential complexity of the relationship between the variables.

  4. Select the Sampling Method: Choose an appropriate sampling method based on the population and available resources. Common sampling methods, such as simple random sampling, stratified sampling, or cluster sampling, can be used for bivariate sampling.

  5. Collect Data: Once the sampling method is determined, collect data on both variables of interest from the chosen individuals or objects in the population. This could involve conducting surveys, interviews, or collecting data from existing sources.

  6. Analyze the Data: Analyze the collected data using appropriate statistical techniques for bivariate analysis. This may involve calculating summary statistics, such as correlation coefficients, conducting regression analysis, or performing hypothesis tests to assess the strength and significance of the relationship between the variables.

Bivariate sampling allows researchers to explore the relationship, association, or dependency between two variables and draw insights into how changes in one variable may impact the other. It is commonly used in various fields, including social sciences, economics, marketing, and epidemiology, where understanding the relationship between two variables is crucial for decision-making or understanding complex phenomena.

It's important to note that bivariate sampling is just one step in exploring the relationship between variables. For more comprehensive analyses, multivariate sampling techniques and advanced statistical methods can be used to investigate relationships involving three or more variables.

Re-Sampling:
  • Resampling is a statistical technique that involves generating new samples from an existing sample to obtain additional information or assess the variability of estimates.
  • It is particularly useful when the sample size is limited or when you want to perform statistical inference without making strong assumptions about the underlying population distribution.
  • There are two common types of resampling methods: bootstrapping and permutation testing (also known as randomization testing).
Estimation:
  • Once a sample is obtained, estimation comes into play. Estimation involves using the data collected from the sample to make statistical inferences and estimate population parameters or characteristics. The population parameters may include means, proportions, totals, or other measures of interest.
  • The goal of estimation is to use the sample data to provide an estimate of the population parameter and quantify the uncertainty associated with that estimate. Estimators, such as sample means, sample proportions, or sample totals, are used to compute the estimates.
  • Statistical methods, such as point estimation and interval estimation, are employed to provide estimates and quantify their precision. Point estimation involves using a single value, such as the sample mean, as an estimate of the population parameter. Interval estimation provides a range of values, known as a confidence interval, within which the population parameter is likely to fall.
