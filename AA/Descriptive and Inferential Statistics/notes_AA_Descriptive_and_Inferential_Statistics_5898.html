Descriptive & Inferential Statistics:
  • Statistics is a branch of mathematics that deals with the collection, analysis, interpretation, presentation, and organization of data.
  • It provides methods and techniques for summarizing and drawing meaningful conclusions from data, helping to make informed decisions and infer insights about populations or phenomena.
  • In statistics, there are two main types: descriptive statistics and inferential statistics. While both types involve analyzing and interpreting data, they differ in their objectives and the manner in which they are applied. Here's a closer look at each type:
Descriptive Statistics:
  • Descriptive statistics involves summarizing, organizing, and presenting data in a meaningful way to provide a clear and concise description of the data set.
  • It focuses on describing the main characteristics and properties of the data, without making inferences or generalizations to a larger population.
  • Descriptive statistics include measures of central tendency (e.g., mean, median, mode) that provide a typical value for the data, measures of variability (e.g., range, variance, standard deviation) that describe the spread or dispersion of the data, and graphical representations such as histograms, bar charts, or pie charts.
  • Descriptive statistics is primarily concerned with gaining insights and understanding patterns within a given dataset, but it does not involve making predictions or drawing conclusions beyond the data itself.
  • Gain insight from historical data with reporting, scorecards, clustering etc. 
  • Can involve data visualization for knowing the basic characteristics of the data 
  • Descriptive analytics answers the questions what happened and why did it happen. 
  • Implementations : Business Intelligence, Visualizations 
  • Software: Informatica, Business Objects, TIBCO Spotfire, Tableau etc.
Inferential Statistics:
  • Inferential statistics goes beyond the data at hand and involves making inferences, generalizations, or predictions about a larger population based on sample data.
  • It involves using statistical techniques to analyze a sample and draw conclusions about the population from which the sample was taken.
  • Inferential statistics allows researchers or analysts to test hypotheses, estimate parameters, and make predictions or statements about the relationship between variables.
  • Key techniques in inferential statistics include hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and regression analysis.
  • By drawing inferences from the sample, inferential statistics provides insights into broader populations, phenomena, or relationships.
Descriptive Statistics:
  • Descriptive statistics is a branch of statistics that involves summarizing, organizing, and presenting data in a meaningful and concise way.
  • It provides a set of tools and techniques to describe the main characteristics, patterns, and properties of a dataset.
  • Descriptive statistics is primarily concerned with understanding and interpreting the data at hand, without making inferences or generalizations to a larger population.

Measures of Central Tendency:
  • Measures of central tendency are statistical measures that provide a single value that represents the center or typical value of a dataset.
  • They help to summarize and describe the central or average value around which the data points are distributed.
  • The three commonly used measures of central tendency are the mean, median, and mode.

​
1. Mean:
  • The mean is the most widely used measure of central tendency.
  • It is calculated by summing up all the values in the dataset and dividing the sum by the total number of observations.
  • The mean is sensitive to extreme values or outliers and can be influenced by skewed distributions. It is represented mathematically as:
                                   
                                    Mean = (Sum of all values) / (Number of observations)

Some other measures of mean used to find the central tendency are as follows:

  • Geometric Mean
  • Harmonic Mean
  • Weighted Mean

Suppose we have a data set representing the daily temperatures (in degrees Celsius) recorded over a week in a particular city:

                                            [22, 24, 21, 20, 23, 25, 23]

To calculate the mean, we add up all the values and divide the sum by the total number of values. In this case, we have 7 temperatures, so:

                                             Mean = (22 + 24 + 21 + 20 + 23 + 25 + 23) / 7

                                             Mean = 158 / 7

                                             Mean ≈ 22.57

Therefore, the mean daily temperature over the week is approximately 22.57 degrees Celsius

​

  • It is observed that if all the values in the dataset are the same, then all geometric, arithmetic and harmonic mean values are the same.
  • If there is variability in the data, then the mean value differs. Calculating the mean value is completely easy. 
2. Median:
  • The median is the middle value in a data set when the values are arranged in ascending or descending order.
  • If there is an odd number of values, the median is the middle value itself. If there is an even number of values, the median is the average of the two middle values.
  • To find the median, the data set needs to be sorted first. If there is an odd number of values, the median can be directly identified as the middle value. If there is an even number of values, the median is calculated as the average of the two middle values.
  • Consider the given dataset with the odd number of observations arranged in descending order – 23, 21, 18, 16, 15, 13, 12, 10, 9, 7, 6, 5, and 2
​

Here 12 is the middle or median number that has 6 values above it and 6 values below it.
Now, consider another example with an even number of observations that are arranged in descending order – 40, 38, 35, 33, 32, 30, 29, 27, 26, 24, 23, 22, 19, and 17

​

When you look at the given dataset, the two middle values obtained are 27 and 29.

Now, find out the mean value for these two numbers.

i.e.,(27+29)/2 =28

Therefore, the median for the given data distribution is 28.

3. Mode:
  • The mode is the value that occurs most frequently in a data set.
  • Unlike the mean and median, which are computed from the actual values, the mode is determined by counting the frequency of each value and identifying the one with the highest frequency.
  • A data set can have no mode (if no value is repeated), or it can have one mode (unimodal), two modes (bimodal), or more (multimodal).
  • The mode is useful for categorical or nominal data, where the values are non-numeric or have distinct categories. It helps identify the most common category or value in the data set. However, the mode does not consider the magnitude of the values, and it is not applicable to continuous numerical data.

Suppose we have a data set representing the scores of students on a math test:

[85, 90, 78, 90, 92, 85, 90, 85, 88, 92]

To find the mode, we need to identify the value(s) that occur most frequently in the data set. In this case, we can see that the numbers 85, 90, and 92 are repeated multiple times.

The mode is the value with the highest frequency. In this example, the number 90 appears three times, which is the highest frequency. Therefore, the mode of the data set is 90.

It is worth noting that a data set can have multiple modes if multiple values have the same highest frequency. However, in this particular example, there is only one mode, which is 90.

The mode provides insight into the most common score among the students. In this case, the mode suggests that 90 is the score that occurred most frequently on the math test.

Measure of Dispersion:
  • Measures of variability, also known as measures of dispersion, quantify the spread, dispersion, or variability of a dataset.
  • They provide information about the extent to which data points deviate from the central tendency. Measures of variability help assess the diversity, consistency, or volatility of the data points. Here are some commonly used measures of variability:
1. Range:
  • The range is the simplest measure of variability. It is the difference between the maximum and minimum values in a dataset.
  • While it provides a quick measure of the spread, it is sensitive to outliers and may not provide a comprehensive understanding of the data's variability.

Suppose we have a dataset representing the daily temperatures (in degrees Celsius) recorded in a city over a week:

                                                              10, 12, 15, 11, 13, 16, 9

To find the range, we need to calculate the difference between the maximum and minimum values in the dataset.

The maximum value in the dataset is 16, and the minimum value is 9.

                                              Range = Maximum value - Minimum value 

                                              Range = 16 - 9 = 7

Therefore, the range of the dataset is 7 degrees Celsius.

2. Variance:
  • The variance is a measure of the average squared deviation of each data point from the mean.
  • It quantifies the spread of the data around the mean.
  • Variance is calculated by taking the sum of the squared differences between each data point and the mean, and then dividing it by the number of observations.
  • The formula for variance is:

                            Variance = Sum of ((Data point - Mean)^2) / Number of observations

Suppose we have a dataset representing the daily temperatures (in degrees Celsius) recorded in a city over a week:

                                                  10, 12, 15, 11, 13, 16, 9

To calculate the variance, we follow these steps:

  1. Calculate the mean of the dataset:
                                   Mean = (10 + 12 + 15 + 11 + 13 + 16 + 9) / 7 = 86 / 7 ≈ 12.29

  2. Calculate the difference between each data point and the mean, squared:
                                                   (10 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 4.9049
                                                   (12 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 0.0841
                                                   (15 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 7.3241
                                                   (11 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 1.6649
                                                   (13 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 0.5329
                                                   (16 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 13.7121
                                                   (9 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 10.7849

  3. Calculate the sum of the squared differences:
    Sum of squared differences = 4.9049 + 0.0841 + 7.3241 + 1.6649 + 0.5329 + 13.7121 + 10.7849                                                                            ≈  39.0079

  4. Divide the sum of squared differences by the number of observations to obtain the variance:
    Variance = Sum of squared differences / Number of observations
                     = 39.0079 / 7
                     ≈ 5.5726

Therefore, the variance of the dataset is approximately 5.5726 square degrees Celsius.

3. Standard Deviation:
  • The standard deviation is the square root of the variance. It measures the average deviation of the data points from the mean.
  • Standard deviation provides a more interpretable measure of variability compared to the variance because it is in the same unit as the data.
  • It is widely used due to its popularity and interpretability.
  • The formula for standard deviation is:

                                      Standard Deviation = Square Root of Variance
Let's continue using the same dataset of daily temperatures (in degrees Celsius) recorded in a city over a week:

                                               10, 12, 15, 11, 13, 16, 9

To calculate the standard deviation, we can follow these steps:

  1. Calculate the mean of the dataset:
                                      Mean = (10 + 12 + 15 + 11 + 13 + 16 + 9) / 7
                                                  = 86 / 7 ≈ 12.29

  2. Calculate the difference between each data point and the mean, squared:
                                      (10 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 4.9049
                                      (12 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 0.0841
                                      (15 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 7.3241
                                      (11 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 1.6649
                                      (13 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 0.5329
                                      (16 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 13.7121
                                      (9 - 12.29)^2 ≈ 10.7849

  3. Calculate the sum of the squared differences:
    Sum of squared differences = 4.9049 + 0.0841 + 7.3241 + 1.6649 + 0.5329 + 13.7121 + 10.7849
                                                      ≈ 39.0079

  4. Divide the sum of squared differences by the number of observations to obtain the variance:
                           Variance = Sum of squared differences / Number of observations
                                            = 39.0079 / 7 ≈ 5.5726

  5. Take the square root of the variance to obtain the standard deviation:
                            Standard Deviation  = Square Root of Variance
                                                                ≈ Square Root of 5.5726
                                                                ≈ 2.3604

Therefore, the standard deviation of the dataset is approximately 2.3604 degrees Celsius.

4. Quartiles:
  • Quartiles are statistical measures that divide a dataset into four equal parts, each containing a quarter (25%) of the data points.
  • They help analyze the distribution and spread of the data by identifying key points within the dataset. The three quartiles divide the data into the following sections:
i) First Quartile (Q1):
The first quartile, also known as the lower quartile, is the value below which 25% of the data falls. It marks the boundary between the lowest 25% of the dataset and the upper 75%. To find the first quartile, the data must be arranged in ascending order, and it is usually denoted as Q1.
ii) Second Quartile (Q2): 
The second quartile is the median of the dataset. It represents the value below which 50% of the data falls. It divides the dataset into two equal halves, with 50% of the data falling below and 50% above the second quartile. The second quartile is denoted as Q2 and is often used as a measure of central tendency.
iii) Third Quartile (Q3):
The third quartile, also known as the upper quartile, is the value below which 75% of the data falls. It marks the boundary between the lowest 75% of the dataset and the upper 25%. Similar to the first quartile, the third quartile is found by arranging the data in ascending order, and it is denoted as Q3.
5. Inter Quartile Range:
  • The interquartile range is a measure of statistical dispersion.
  • It represents the range between the first quartile (25th percentile) and the third quartile (75th percentile) in a dataset.
  • The IQR is robust to outliers and provides a measure of the spread of the middle 50% of the data.
  • It can be used to identify outliers or assess the spread in skewed distributions.

6. Coefficient of Variation (CV):
  • The coefficient of variation (CV) is a statistical measure that represents the relative variability or dispersion of a dataset in relation to its mean.
  • It is used to compare the variability of datasets with different scales or units.
  • The CV is expressed as a percentage and is calculated by dividing the standard deviation of the dataset by the mean and then multiplying by 100.

The formula for calculating the coefficient of variation is as follows:

                                   CV = (Standard Deviation / Mean) * 100
Let's consider two datasets:

Dataset A: [10, 15, 20, 25, 30]
Dataset B: [50, 55, 60, 65, 70]

For Dataset A:

  1. Calculate the mean:
                                   Mean = (10 + 15 + 20 + 25 + 30) / 5 = 100 / 5 = 20

  2. Calculate the standard deviation:
    Standard Deviation = √[((10 - 20)^2 + (15 - 20)^2 + (20 - 20)^2 + (25 - 20)^2 + (30 - 20)^2) / 5]
                                        = √[(100 + 25 + 0 + 25 + 100) / 5]
                                        = √[250 / 5] = √50 ≈ 7.07

  3. Calculate the coefficient of variation:
                                    CV = (Standard Deviation / Mean) * 100
                                          = (7.07 / 20) * 100 ≈ 35.35

For Dataset B:

  1. Calculate the mean:
                                     Mean = (50 + 55 + 60 + 65 + 70) / 5 = 300 / 5 = 60

  2. Calculate the standard deviation:
    Standard Deviation = √[((50 - 60)^2 + (55 - 60)^2 + (60 - 60)^2 + (65 - 60)^2 + (70 - 60)^2) / 5]
                                       = √[(100 + 25 + 0 + 25 + 100) / 5]
                                       = √[250 / 5] = √50 ≈ 7.07

  3. Calculate the coefficient of variation:
                                       CV = (Standard Deviation / Mean) * 100
                                             = (7.07 / 60) * 100 ≈ 11.78

In this example, Dataset A has a coefficient of variation of approximately 35.35%, while Dataset B has a coefficient of variation of approximately 11.78%. This indicates that Dataset A has a higher relative variability compared to its mean, while Dataset B has a lower relative variability.

Inferential Statistics:​​​​​​​
  • Inferential statistics is a branch of statistics that involves drawing conclusions or making inferences about a population based on data collected from a sample.
  • It utilizes probability theory and statistical principles to generalize findings from a sample to a larger population.
  • The goal of inferential statistics is to make predictions, test hypotheses, estimate parameters, and draw meaningful conclusions about the population based on limited information obtained from the sample.

Inferential statistics involves the following key concepts and techniques:

  1. Sampling: Inferential statistics relies on sampling, where a subset of individuals or items is selected from a larger population of interest. The sample should ideally be representative of the population to ensure that inferences can be made accurately.

  2. Estimation: Estimation involves using sample data to estimate or infer population parameters. For example, the sample mean can be used as an estimate of the population mean. Confidence intervals are often calculated to provide a range of plausible values for the population parameter.

  3. Hypothesis Testing: Hypothesis testing is used to assess whether there is enough evidence to support or reject a specific hypothesis about the population. It involves formulating a null hypothesis (H₀) and an alternative hypothesis (H₁), collecting data, and using statistical tests to evaluate the evidence against the null hypothesis.

  4. Statistical Tests: Various statistical tests are used in inferential statistics to analyze the sample data and draw conclusions about the population. Common tests include t-tests, chi-square tests, analysis of variance (ANOVA), correlation analysis, and regression analysis. These tests help determine whether observed differences or relationships in the sample are statistically significant and can be generalized to the population.

  5. Probability: Probability theory is a fundamental aspect of inferential statistics. It provides a mathematical framework for quantifying uncertainty and calculating the likelihood of events occurring. Probability distributions, such as the normal distribution, are often used to model random variables and make inferences.

  6. Type I and Type II Errors: In inferential statistics, there is a risk of making errors. A Type I error occurs when the null hypothesis is rejected when it is actually true. A Type II error occurs when the null hypothesis is accepted when it is actually false. These errors are controlled by setting a significance level (alpha) and calculating p-values, which indicate the probability of observing the data if the null hypothesis is true.
